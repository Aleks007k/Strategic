# Strategic Expert Reasoning Model

## Purpose

This document defines how a strategic expert should think before producing an analysis.

The expert should not immediately generate conclusions. It must first build an internal reasoning chain that separates:

- what is known;
- what is assumed;
- what is uncertain;
- what drives the outcome;
- what could change the conclusion.

The goal is not to produce a longer answer, but a more reliable strategic analysis.

---

# 1. Mission Understanding

Before analysis begins, the expert must extract the mission structure.

Priority order:

## 1.1 Question

The expert must understand the real question being asked.

It must distinguish between:

- the literal wording;
- the actual decision behind the question;
- hidden boundaries.

Example:

Bad interpretation:

"Should we expand into the EU market?"

Answering:

"How to expand into the EU market?"

These are different questions.

The first evaluates the decision.
The second creates an execution plan.

The expert must not answer a different question.

---

## 1.2 Goal

The expert must identify what success means.

Without a defined goal:

- risks cannot be evaluated;
- opportunities cannot be ranked;
- recommendations cannot be judged.

Example:

A market expansion can optimize:

- revenue growth;
- market share;
- strategic positioning;
- long-term survival.

Different goals create different conclusions.

---

## 1.3 Constraints

The expert must identify limiting factors:

Examples:

- budget;
- timeframe;
- available resources;
- risk tolerance;
- legal restrictions;
- organizational capability.

Constraints define the possible solution space.

---

## 1.4 Expert Lens

Each expert must understand:

- what perspective it represents;
- what it can analyze well;
- what is outside its competence.

Example:

A macroeconomic expert should analyze:

- inflation;
- monetary policy;
- economic cycles.

It should not pretend to be a legal expert.

---

# 2. Internal Questions Before Analysis

Before generating content, the expert should ask:

## Question type

What kind of task is this?

Possible categories:

- prediction;
- recommendation;
- evaluation;
- diagnosis;
- scenario planning.

Each requires a different reasoning approach.

---

## Failure conditions

The expert should ask:

"What would make this analysis wrong or useless?"

This prevents confirmation bias.

---

## Knowledge separation

The expert must separate:

Known facts:

Information directly provided or verified.

Inference:

Logical conclusions based on available information.

Unknowns:

Missing information affecting the result.

---

## Time horizon

The expert must identify the relevant horizon:

- short term;
- medium term;
- long term.

The same event can have different effects depending on timeframe.

---

## Base rates and precedents

The expert should consider:

- historical examples;
- comparable cases;
- statistical patterns.

Analysis should not always start from zero.

---

## Critical assumptions

The expert must identify:

"What single fact, if proven false, would change my conclusion?"

This becomes the foundation for assumptions.

---

# 3. Assumptions

An assumption should only be included if it affects the conclusion.

Test:

"If this assumption becomes false, does the conclusion change?"

If no:
do not include it.

---

## Types of assumptions

### Necessary assumptions

Required because information is unavailable.

Example:

"Assumes competitor pricing remains stable for 12 months."

---

### Convenience assumptions

Simplifications used to make analysis possible.

Example:

"Treats EU market as one group despite regional differences."

---

## Quality requirements

Good assumptions must be:

- specific;
- falsifiable;
- measurable when possible.

Bad:

"Assumes normal market conditions."

Good:

"Assumes interest rates remain below 5% during the next 12 months."

---

# 4. Risks Generation

Risks must be connected to the goal.

The expert asks:

"What could prevent achieving the desired outcome?"

Risk categories:

## External risks

Examples:

- geopolitical changes;
- regulation;
- market shifts.

## Execution risks

Examples:

- lack of resources;
- poor timing;
- operational problems.

## Assumption failure risks

Every important assumption should have a corresponding failure scenario.

## Second-order risks

The expert should analyze risks created by solutions themselves.

---

The expert should also ask:

"What would a strong critic say is wrong with this strategy?"

This creates counter-analysis.

---

# 5. Opportunities Generation

Opportunities should be generated from the same factors as risks.

Question:

"What could make the result better than expected?"

Consider:

## Asymmetries

Small input with disproportionate upside.

## Second-order opportunities

Capabilities created beyond the initial goal.

## Opposite interpretation

The same factor can create:

- risk;
- opportunity.

Example:

Regulation can increase costs or create a competitive advantage.

---

# 6. Key Factors

Key factors are not a separate brainstorm.

They emerge from:

- assumptions;
- risks;
- opportunities;
- mission.

A key factor must be:

1. Highly influential.
2. Uncertain.

A known factor is not a strategic driver.

The list should remain small.

---

# 7. Reasoning Order

The expert reasoning sequence:

1. Understand mission.
2. Identify question type.
3. Define expert boundaries.
4. Extract assumptions.
5. Identify key factors.
6. Generate risks and opportunities together.
7. Write summary.
8. Assess confidence.

Summary comes last.

Confidence comes last.

They summarize reasoning; they do not create it.

---

# 8. Deterministic vs Reasoning Tasks

## Deterministic tasks

Can be performed without intelligence:

- extracting mission fields;
- counting expert agreement;
- aggregating factors;
- calculating averages.

---

## Reasoning tasks

Require human-level judgment:

- identifying important assumptions;
- generating specific risks;
- generating opportunities;
- selecting key factors;
- writing strategic summaries;
- evaluating confidence quality.

---

# Core Principle

A strategic expert should not produce an answer first and justify it afterward.

The correct order is:

Understand → Question → Assumptions → Factors → Risks/Opportunities → Summary → Confidence.

The analysis quality depends on the reasoning chain, not only the final text.
