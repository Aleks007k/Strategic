# Expert Reasoning Process

## Purpose

Define how a strategic expert should think before producing an AnalysisResult.

The goal is not fast answer generation.
The goal is disciplined reasoning.

## Reasoning Order

The expert follows this sequence:

1. Understand the mission.
2. Define the analytical frame.
3. Identify assumptions.
4. Identify key factors.
5. Generate risks and opportunities.
6. Write the summary.
7. Assess confidence.

## 1. Mission Understanding

Before analysis, the expert extracts:

### Question

The real question being asked.

The expert must distinguish:

- what is explicitly asked;
- what is implicitly required;
- what is outside the scope.

### Goal

What success means.

Without a clear goal:

- risks cannot be evaluated;
- opportunities cannot be prioritized.

### Constraints

Important limitations:

- budget;
- time;
- resources;
- acceptable risk level.

### Expert Lens

The expert must understand:

- what perspective it provides;
- what areas belong to other experts.

An expert should avoid analyzing outside its competence.

## 2. Internal Framing Questions

Before producing conclusions:

- Is this prediction, recommendation, or evaluation?
- What could make this analysis wrong?
- What is known versus assumed?
- What time horizon applies?
- Are there historical precedents?
- What single fact could change the conclusion?

## 3. Assumption Identification

Only important assumptions should be included.

A load-bearing assumption is one where:

"If this becomes false, the conclusion changes."

Types:

### Necessity assumptions

Information is unavailable and must be assumed.

Example:

"Competitor pricing remains stable for 12 months."

### Convenience assumptions

Simplifications made to keep analysis manageable.

Example:

"EU market treated as one segment."

Assumptions should be:

- specific;
- falsifiable;
- connected to conclusions.

## 4. Risk Generation

Risks are not generic lists.

The expert asks:

"What could prevent achieving the goal?"

Risk categories:

- external/environmental;
- execution;
- assumption failure;
- second-order consequences.

The expert should also ask:

"What would a skeptical reviewer challenge?"

## 5. Opportunity Generation

Opportunities are generated from the same factors as risks.

The expert asks:

"What could make the outcome better than expected?"

Consider:

- asymmetric upside;
- second-order benefits;
- strategic advantages.

The same factor can create both risk and opportunity.

## 6. Key Factors

Key factors emerge from risks, opportunities, and assumptions.

A factor is important when:

- it strongly influences the outcome;
- it remains uncertain.

The list should remain small.

## 7. Summary Creation

Summary is written last.

It compresses the reasoning chain.

It should not be the starting hypothesis.

## 8. Confidence Assessment

Confidence is evaluated after reasoning.

It reflects:

- quality of information;
- dependency on assumptions;
- uncertainty.

Confidence should not be calculated before analysis exists.

## Deterministic vs Reasoning Tasks

Deterministic:

- extracting mission fields;
- aggregating expert outputs;
- counting agreement.

Reasoning:

- selecting assumptions;
- generating risks;
- generating opportunities;
- identifying key factors;
- writing conclusions.
